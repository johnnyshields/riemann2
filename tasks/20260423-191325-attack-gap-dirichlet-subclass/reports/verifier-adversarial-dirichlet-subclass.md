Claim

The current record does not support the unqualified claim that primitive nonprincipal complex characters with fixed parity are the cleanest first Dirichlet subclass. The strongest safe conclusion is narrower: from current scope alone, for an exact paired-source theorem specifically, the most natural first subclass remains primitive nonprincipal complex characters, and a fixed-parity restriction is at most a statement-level simplifier. Real primitive nonprincipal characters remain a live rival subclass because self-duality may collapse some front-end bookkeeping, but no supplied note proves that this collapse reaches the exact source-to-slot burden.

Status

open

Evidence

Proved from the supplied notes.

- The project-wide priority note ranks the `primitive Dirichlet paired-scalar exact-source theorem` as the best first non-zeta realization target only `from current scope alone`; it does not rank a finer subclass inside primitive Dirichlet, and it does not claim that one parity is analytically superior. That already forces scoped wording.
- The paired-source note identifies the safe paired object and exactly what pairing fixes: scalar reality/sign safety, cleaner boundary-phase packaging, and disappearance of the residual root number. The same note is equally explicit that pairing does not fix the theorem-sized source gap, local-slot identification, denominator comparability, corrected whitening, odd/transverse realization, or boundary control. So any subclass recommendation can only be about front-end naturalness unless more is proved.
- The Dirichlet-channel note repeats that the paired `((\chi,\bar\chi))` route is the more conservative first scalar target, not a proved safer route. It also says the honest statement is only a candidate-selection statement. That blocks stronger language like `cleanest first subclass` if that phrase is meant to imply shortest proved path.
- Route A is directionally right that complex characters are the subclass where pairing does real structural work, while in the real/self-dual case the paired object collapses toward a square and may no longer be the most natural formulation. But Route A overreaches when it ends with an unqualified verdict that complex characters `are the cleanest first subclass`; current notes do not prove that the real self-dual route fails to overtake them once the full source theorem is counted.
- Route B is closer to the adversarial boundary. It correctly separates front-end cleanliness from the still-missing exact source-to-slot package and treats real/self-dual characters as only a cleaner candidate-selection subclass, not a proved better theorem target.

Conditional on standard primitive-Dirichlet facts.

- For a real primitive nonprincipal character, `\chi=\bar\chi`, so the paired package loses its main non-self-dual purpose. This supports the narrower statement that complex characters are the cleanest first subclass for a paired theorem, because pairing is actually resolving something there.
- Fixing parity should simplify only the gamma-factor bookkeeping in the theorem statement by freezing the archimedean case split. No supplied note shows that it removes the main analytic burdens behind exact local-slot identification.

Missing.

- A head-to-head theorem comparison between the real/self-dual route and the paired-complex route on the same checklist: object choice, background identification, exact `S(m)`-slot realization, convergence/regularization, denominator control, whitening, and odd/transverse transport.
- Any proof that fixed parity does more than simplify presentation.
- Any proof that the self-dual real subclass automatically collapses enough hidden local bookkeeping to beat the paired-complex theorem overall.
- The exact paired source theorem itself, even in the complex subclass.

Strongest objection

The strongest objection is that this verdict may still understate the real/self-dual subclass. If self-duality forces not just front-end simplification but also the correct background normalization, exact local-slot packaging, or simpler denominator and odd-package transport, then real primitive characters could be the genuinely cleanest first subclass even for realized theorems. Current scope does not prove that. But current scope also does not prove the opposite. So the safe line is not `complex with fixed parity is the cleanest subclass`; it is `complex is the cleanest first subclass for the paired theorem-facing formulation from current scope alone`.

Needed for promotion

1. Replace `cleanest first subclass` by `cleanest first subclass for an exact paired-source theorem from current scope alone` wherever the broader wording appears.
2. Replace `fixed parity` claims that sound structural by `fixed parity is a theorem-statement simplifier unless further analytic savings are proved`.
3. Scope-weaken any sentence that sounds like proof-length reduction:
   `complex paired route is the cleanest first subclass` -> `for the paired theorem-facing formulation, primitive nonprincipal complex characters are the most natural first subclass from current scope alone`.
4. Scope-weaken any sentence that treats real characters as displaced:
   `real primitive characters are not the right first subclass` -> `real primitive characters do not yet overtake the paired-complex route from current scope alone, but remain a live rival if the project leaves the paired formulation or if self-duality collapses more bookkeeping than is currently proved`.
5. Scope-weaken any sentence that credits parity with substantive progress:
   `fixed parity reduces the main burden` -> `fixed parity freezes the archimedean case split and may simplify statement bookkeeping; no deeper reduction is currently proved`.
6. Honest verdict: primitive nonprincipal complex characters with fixed parity are not proved to be the unqualified cleanest first subclass. The safe current wording is narrower. For the paired-source theorem-facing program, complex nonprincipal primitive characters remain the most natural first subclass from current scope alone, and fixed parity is only a scoped presentation simplifier. The broader subclass conclusion should be weakened.
