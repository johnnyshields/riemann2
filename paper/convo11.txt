What your draft is trying to do, at the level of approach, is fairly distinctive. It is not mainly a classical explicit-formula attack, not mainly a de Branges/positivity attack, not mainly a Hilbert–Pólya spectral attack, and not mainly a random-matrix/local-statistics attack. Instead it builds a local two-point jet-normalized matrix object from the phase kernel, compares that to an explicit off-line toy anomaly, kills the leading value channel with a chosen transverse functional, then calibrates the remaining size using the actual value-channel derivative, and finally uses an odd holomorphic NN-point cancellation to force the zeta-side transverse scalar to become exponentially small. As a package, that has a real “new synthesis” feel.

That said, it does not feel like it came out of nowhere. Several nearby instincts have definitely been tried before, in serious ways. Kernel/Hilbert-space/reproducing-kernel approaches are part of the RH landscape; de Branges-type positivity conditions and related reproducing-kernel structures have been studied explicitly, and Conrey–Li already highlighted real difficulties with pushing de Branges positivity directly toward RH. The broader RH literature also contains large families of function-space, positivity, and dynamical/spectral approaches.

Likewise, anything that leans on local rescaling near height TT sits near a very well-developed tradition around local zero statistics. Modern surveys emphasize that one must rescale locally because zero density varies with height, and this local viewpoint is central to the Montgomery/Odlyzko/Katz–Sarnak world. But that is also exactly why a purely local-statistical attack is dangerous: Lagarias and Rodgers showed that what is currently known about local statistics does not rule out an “alternative hypothesis” point process mimicking those statistics.

That is why your draft feels different from a mere spacing-statistics argument. It is trying to use more rigid analytic structure than statistics alone: not just local spacing data, but a specific normalized block built from the phase derivative, a specific transverse annihilator, and a specific calibrated odd-jet mechanism. In other words, it is trying to escape the “local statistics are too soft” objection by introducing a much more structured local invariant. That part does feel nonstandard.

There is also an older geometric ancestry here. Any RH route that extracts local geometry from ζζ, ξξ, or their derivatives lives in the shadow of Speiser-type phenomena: RH is equivalent to a certain geometric restriction on zeros of ζ′(s)ζ′(s), and that has long encouraged people to look for geometric encodings of off-line zeros. But your draft is not just “another Speiser reformulation.” The jet-whitening plus toy-anomaly comparison is more bespoke than that.

\Delta_{\toy}(u,d;s)

\qquad

\]

\begin{proof}

off-line perturbation of the toy block. By construction it depends smoothly
(in fact analytically) on the parameters $(u,s,d)$ in a neighborhood of
\[
(u,s,d)=(0,0,d_0),
\qquad d_0\in D.
\]

split there is no linear term in $u$. Hence the first nontrivial term is
quadratic:
\[
\Delta_{\toy}(u,d;s)=u^2M(d,s)+O(u^4),
\]
uniformly on compact $d$-ranges and for $|s|$ in a fixed microscopic disk.

By the jet-limit computation for the toy model, the leading quadratic
coefficient is independent of the microscopic node $s$ and equals the toy
anomaly matrix $M(d)$. Thus
\[
M(d,s)\equiv M(d),
\]
and the expansion becomes
\[
\Delta_{\toy}(u,d;s)=u^2M(d)+u^4E(u,d;s),
\]
with $E$ uniformly bounded on compact parameter ranges. Restricting to the
microscopic nodes $s_j=j/Q^2$ gives the stated uniform bound for
$E_j(u,d)$.
\end{proof}
